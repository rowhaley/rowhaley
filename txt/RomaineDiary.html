Bob Whaley’s Diary of Romaine River Trip August 1978


Phil Leider (leader), Tom Cole, Larry Gross, Dave Bowen, Tom Coron, Paul Ferguson


Sunday August 13th 5:05pm

I am sitting on the Romaine river 120 miles from its mouth. The river here is about 1/4 to 1/2 a mile across and spruce forest cover the hills that rise about 400 feet from the river. At this place the river looks like a large mountain lake. The waves are lapping at the shore. I am wearing a bug jacket though temperature is about 80F. I expect to eat in about one hour and then go to bed.

To Backtrack:

Left home 4:30 on Friday August 11th 1978. Arrived at Larry’s house 6:20. Left Larry’s around 7. Picked up Phil in Columbia Md. Larry’s van had a bad fan belt. Lost 1 hour fixing it. Picked up Tom Cole 11:00 - 2 hours late. My car cut off in the rain about ¾ of an hour later. C.B was a big help in notifying Larry. After a short time it started again. It cut off again in upper New York State. We found a loose wire. It seems OK now.

We lost some time in a small town outside Montreal trying to get a fishing license. Tried again in Quebec City, got lost again. Still no fishing license.

Arrived in Harve St Pierre at 6am Sunday morning; had breakfast and talked to air line about flying in and rivers. All rivers are low.

We decided to do Romaine. Put in was to be determined on first flight in.

We had decided to fly in on Monday but decided to go in today at the suggestion of the airline.

Jack Easterling did not make trip.

I am real tired but still keyed up about this trip. We will start down river tomorrow.

Monday August 14th

My group job is to prepare breakfast. A good one for me since I am usually up early. I got up around 6:30. Phil Leider in charge of fires was still asleep. I gathered tender from a large birch near our campsite and got the fire started and put on water for coffee and laid out cereal products.

Last night I slept very well – went to bed about 7:30. We did have a bunch of mosquitoes in the tent and Paul Ferguson did do some snoring.

We paddled about ½ a mile down stream and had to portage a large water fall about 15 feet. The portage not at all bad. Below the falls was a beautiful sand beach bordered by extensive outcroppings of rock.

We ate lunch, some fished, some hiked – no fish caught for lunch. I hooked and lost 2 very small trout.

We canoed to the end of this pond, made another portage then canoed with a strong wind at our back about four additional miles. I stuck two paddles up into my cagoule for a sail. Larry used his paddle at a rudder and we really moved out.

The wind is still blowing very fast.

With the black flies, mosquitoes and my small bladder, I am very glad I brought along my P jug.

After lunch today I sat down on a log overlooking the falls and had a very satisfactory bowel movement in the prettiest of all toilets.

Tomorrow we expect to have about 12 miles of easy paddling and then reach a very rough 5 mile section that may take as much as 3 days.

Tuesday August 15 7pm

Cloudy, windy, looks like rain.

Last night was cold. The temperature dropped to 42F. Today was windy, very cloudy and we had some rain, perhaps as much as ½ inch.

Phil Leider and Tom Cole decided to get up early and climb a small mountain along a small stream that apparently was feed from a lake at the top..

The rest of us slept late and paddled down river to within hearing distance of a big falls. What should be the roughest part of the trip should be coming up. Judging from the scouting trips in by plane.

We are again camped again on a sand bar.

Tom Cole came back with mosquito bites so thick he looked like a teen age boy with acne.

We stopped today at what appeared to be an abandoned hunting camp. Old cans had food that appeared to still be good. I picked up a aluminum pot that I hope to get home to remind me of this trip.

Helicopters keep coming up and down the river taking away from the experience. They come close and look. At one point today I thought they were going to land.

Apparently they are making a survey of this river for a possible dam.

I am a little fearful of tomorrow but if we use good judgment everything should be OK.

Friday August 18 7pm

Clear, getting cool.

I can’t remember what happened on Wed except we ran into work crews from Hydro Quebec. They had detailed maps of the next three miles. These are now behind us.

Thursday we made a portage of about ½ mile and today was made one of close to a mile. I doubt if we have covered as much as 4 miles in the last two days.

Until we made camp tonight spirits were not very high. Tomorrow with a paddle of about ¼ mile - a short portage and who knows what is next.

In spite of the rain and the hard work I am enjoying this experience very much. The scenery has been spectacular and the mosquitoes and black flies beyond description.

I took a swim the first day. Since then I have not taken a bath and probably won’t.

Saturday August 19

Weather clear, warm, blue sky, lovely scenery.

Day started slow. We ate pancakes for breakfast. Paddled about 1/8 mile then did a short easy portage. Paddled about another mile. Spent about two hours scouting a long rapid, ran the rapid, pulled up to scout another rapid and decided to make camp.

Everyone washed and dried clothes and now it is time for supper.

I expect we are going to be pushed to get out.

I feel I am very luck to have this experience, although it is very tough.

Sunday August 20 7pm

Weather bright and clear. Rained most of the day.

Today we made 3 portages. One about ½ a mile and two about ¼ of a mile each. All made across rocks.

We are camped on a sand beach. Across the river is a big rock hill maybe 600 feet high, rugged and beautiful. A rapid is above and below our camp site and the water between is moving.

We have covered about 23 miles since we put in. Everyone healthy and spirits are good.

Monday August 21 7pm

Weather clear and beautiful. Camp is set up.

We ate 5 small brook trout in addition to our regular supper. They were delicious. I think I particularly enjoyed them since I had caught the two largest.

Last night was very cold. Today we paddled about ¼ of a mile, made a short portage. Then another short portage. Then a tough ¼ mile portage across rocks through woods – uphill - lining across a creek and finally dragging down a creek bed.

Tempers were high during portage but everyone seems OK now.

After the portage we paddled 4-5 miles found a lovely camping spot and stopped around 3:30.

We loafed, took baths, washed clothes, fished and had supper.

We are now entering a slower section of the river so we should make more distance each day. I think the toughest part is over. We will soon be to the section of this river that has been paddled before.

Tuesday August 22 5pm

Weather hot and windy

Paddled 18 miles today. Tomorrow we will hit rapids at the narrows going into the basin. This I guess will take about two days. We probably will have a rest day in the basin. I am more tried today than any day. I think it is the glare.

This is certainly a big powerful river but this section today was easy except the wind blew in our faces all day.

Wednesday August 23

We ran five rapids about 12 miles. The morning was cloudy, it rained, now it is clear, cold, and very windy.

I have put rocks on all my tent stakes and tied a line to it to help hold it against the wind.

Several days ago I fell and hit my elbow. I put a band aid over the small cut. When I removed the bandaid today a lot of clear fluid squirted out. It doesn’t hurt and it is not red.

Tomorrow we should start into the Les Murailles section (the walls).

We ran across more of the dam builder crews today and a helicopter landed across the river from our camp. I wish they were on another river.

Thursday August 24

Windy and cold.

Last night just as I finished writing a big gust of wind blew my tent down. The tent is OK. I had to straighten several bent poles.

We made about 4 (I think) short portages and one the book says is 300 kilometers [sic].

A helicopter landed in the rocks by our camp and one of the dam builders got out and came over and talked, while the pilot and another man fished. They said the Romaine is the worst fishing river in this area. The man from the helicopter gave Tom Coron some tobacco. Tom had run out several days earlier. Tom was most elated.

My elbow has been squirting a yellow fluid. I suspect I have punctured my fluid sac. It is tender but does not hurt.

Friday August 25

Today we made 3 portages, some lining, and a tough ¾ mile portage into the basin,

Tom Cole and I cut the portage trail.

The above is being written on Saturday, Aug 26 at 10am. We are camped in the Basin and sort of catching up.

I suspect we will leave here around 11am and camp below a falls 13 miles down stream. Probably we will do 2 short portages today.

I bumped my elbow again yesterday. It is tender and swollen some. I think this elbow is taking some of my strength, but I am keeping up pretty good.

My fingers are cracked from the sand, wind, water, and cold, but have improved some. I should have used hand cream from the start.

Sat August 26th 6:30pm

Clear. Temperature about 60F. Wind very strong from noon on.

We are camped above two water falls separated by a runnable rapid The whole river necks down to about 30 feet and is most impressive.

We did run one rapid up stream that the guide book said to line on the left. We are now 32 miles from the take out.

Sunday August 27th

Beautiful day. Beautiful spot.

Temperature hot, no wind, and clear.

We made 4 portages today and covered about 7 miles. We are camped on a moss covered rock high above the river with a beautiful view of Grand Chute – a very impressive 90 ft waterfall. Down stream is a rapid type of drop that we will have to portage the first thing in the morning.

We have approximately 30 miles to go, mostly flat, separated by waterfalls.

My elbow is the same, swollen and squirts. It is tender, but does not hurt. My hands are getting better.

It is nice to be off the sand for one night.

I didn’t mention earlier, but it is nice to eat blue berries on almost all portages.

Monday August 28 7pm

Beautiful day, clear and crisp. Frost last night.

I got up to P last night and enjoyed the Northern Light display, the view of the falls and the crisp night air.

Made one short portage. Paddled 10 miles of flat water and ate lunch at Charlies Chute. Loafed, fished – no luck.

Saw evidence of Indian camp and fish netting and salmon drying.

Our tent is on a sand bar. The door opens to a beautiful view of Charlies Chute - a 15 foot drop with an island in the center – beautiful.

Phil is counting portages. He says 26 so far.

We expect to paddle about 10 miles tomorrow, make one portage, find a camp site and loaf.

The next day we expect to get up early and paddle out. Dave, Tom Coron and Paul Ferguson are getting tired of the river and want very much to take off.

I don’t want to take off, but I would like to catch one good fish and see a moose, some wolves and a bear.

Wednesday August 30 10:30am

Harve St Pierre. Clear windy and cold.

Yesterday we broke camp in a drizzle. With the wind at our back and the itch to get moving, we covered the first ten miles by 11:30am.

We started a fire beside a railroad bridge support to warm ourselves from the wind, rain and cool and started to eat lunch. The fire caused moisture expansion in the concrete which started breaking off, throwing rocks and sand at us – no place for lunch.

We finished lunch in a filthy dirty railroad line shack – it was warm but the most uncomfortable time on the trip for me.

The condition of things indicated the judgment of – get off today.

Larry and I paddled out ahead of the group. He watched the canoes and I caught a ride into town with a man who wouldn’t or couldn’t speak English. He made no effort to communicate. Interesting ride.

I packed up my van at the police station drove back. We checked into a motel. I don’t believe I am as glad to be off the river as the rest.

The beer and the shower were fine. Rock music from the bar downstairs kept me awake most of the night.
