A Man for All Seasons

By Maurice Greaver

Coastal CaNEWS Fall 2023

Bob Whaley pulled into his last take out of life on July 4th, 2023 and relaxed into eternity at 97 years old. How fitting. Having served our nation in WWII defending our independence, that he left this earth on July 4th. Life well done, Bob. RIP. Our paddling community sends our condolences to Bob’s family and friends.

Bob began paddling in the late 1960s (in his 40s) and continued until 2010 (age 84). I often told him he was my hero for padding good white water into his 80s. To do this, Bob worked out a the gym several times each week. He also enjoyed nature, swimming, camping, fishing, scuba diving and cross-county skiing. I first saw Bob at my first (April 1978) Coastals club meeting when I (then age 26) noticed the man with the full head of white hair (he was only 52 then). Bob enjoyed all types of rivers, and considered the Maury River (Goshen Pass), Tye River, and the Moormans River as his home rivers.

Bob had a unique way to record his river exploits, by filling in the river sections with ink on a topo map (primarily of Virginia, West Virginia, and North Carolina) pasted to an inside wall of his house in Charlottesville. Bob enjoyed paddling entire rivers, including the Potomac River from the headwaters n WV/MD to Washington DC as well as both branches of the Shenandoah River into the Potomac. That required paddling many miles of flatwater, and Olie Fordham joined him on many of those trips. I often told Bob I appreciated he and Ollie taking those trips so I didn’t have to.

Bob was an excellent technical paddler. John Butcher said, “Bob paddled with fewer strokes than anybody else I’ve ever seen.” As a result, few paddlers ever got to see the Whale take a swim. We were paddling in Quebec, on the Rouge River’s Harrington Canyon section. Bob hit the horizon line above a 20-foot slide into a large hole, but appeared to jump out of his boat (nobody’s perfect) just beyond the horizon line. In the eddy below, Butcher and I could not believe it. After reviewing the raft company videos several times at their outpost, John coined the term “premature ejumpulation” to describe Bob’s canoe exit. Bob found the description quite humorous.

Good paddling partners are flexible and realistic, in a sport where water levels, weather, and rapid difficulties can very widely. Bob was that. Ollie Fordham, Don Bowman, the Whale, my son Brad, a client, and I were rafting the Lower Gauley in my 14’ Solar. It was warm and we were having a great day hitting the lines. At the top of “Pure Screaming Hell”, I suggested that we vote on whether to run or miss the big hole, and the majority should rule. The vote was 4-2 for running it with Bob and Don against. I explained what was necessary for success. With that crew, that day. I gave us a 95% chance for a clean run. We pulled together, were right on line, had great momentum when we hit the hole, and powered through it, drenched but unscathed. We were all ecstatic. The smiles of achievement on Bob’s and Don’s faces were priceless.

Bob had a great sense of humor. John Butcher recounted one such moment on Chattooga IV. “Down at Tagaloo Like, after the whitewater, we faced the one-hour paddle out across the lake in the heat. Whaley noticed the NOC raft trip: they tie the rafts to a skiff and take a powered trip down the lake to the takeout. Whale paddled up and surfed the wake of this abomination until the guide took mercy and grabbed the bow of his canoe. We watched Whaley coast away, mopped our brows, cursed him, and paddled into the heat. When we arrived at the takeout, sweaty and exhausted, Whaley was sitting in the shade with a can of the Breakfast of Champions, laughing.” Bob had the smirk of the cat that are the canary, enjoying his triumph.

Brad Graver (then 16) added another look at Bob’s sense of humor, telling the following story. “Don and I were paddling the Shredder in Tennessee on the Big Pigeon River. Dad and Bob took a break and pulled their canoes up on the bank, to make room for Don and I to pull in. My side of the Shredder was on the shallow bank side so I could put my foot in the water to stand up, stop the boat and keep if from moving further down the bank in the fast-moving current. For some reason Don stood up as we pulled in. Well I took that opportunity to rapidly exit the raft and the entire weight of Don’s body became the only weight in the Shredder. He quickly fell overboard. Bob nearly busted a gut laughing at Don from the bank. The water was cold, so Don wasn’t laughing, which made Bob laugh even harder.

Bob had such a positive spirit. One day in early March, Bob and I were paddling the Gauley River’s Crupperneck/Donnigan Mine sections. Before Summersville Lake was refilled as part of the annual flood control. We put in at the Cherry River confluence and planned to take out at the boat landing on the lake West of the Route 19 bridge. The trip to the Route 39 bridge went smoothly, but as so often happens in WV – weather changes. The warm windless day turned into a cold, 50 mph upstream gale wind. We struggled to make any headway across the lake. We alternated paddling and walking on the muddy banks of the drawn down lake. I gritted my teeth and grumbled. Bob smiled and encouraged. It took another 2+ hours to go less than the couple of miles to the take-out as dusk approached.

Those who paddled with Bob had great respect for him as a person. Bob Card explained. “My father and I had a great relationship and we both enjoyed the outdoors. Several months after my father died, I began to call Bob Whaley ‘Dad’. It just felt right.” The Whale had many paddling buddies, including the following (in alphabetical order), whom I could remember: Tom Blue, Don Bowman, Scott Broaddus, Neil Burnett, John Butcher, Bob Card, Tome Cole, Andre Derdeyn, John DeBoer, Paul Ferguson, Ollie Fordham, Les Fry, Liz Garland, Russell Goin, Bob Gramann, Maurice and Brad Greaver, Larry and Christy Gross, Lou Hannen, Dwight Holton, Nate Houser, Alicia Jahsmann, Phil Leider, Chris Leonard, Gill and Doug Lipton, Tom, Tom Jr.. John, Ned and George Michie, Chip Queitzsch, Conny Roussos, Glen Rose, DeWitt Smith, Dave Stockdill, Steve Thomas, John and Linda Van Luik, Steve Wagner, Jenny Wiley, and Sandy Williams. (I’m sorry that I probably missed some.) I suspect all of them, have good Whaley stories to tell … and all will miss him. Doug Lipton summed up his thoughts about Bob, going back to his paddling days as a young teenager, “Bob’s sense of humor, his wit, and his warm smile that exuded kindness, were exceptional traits to be bundled up into just one person. Every interaction I had with him was a positive experience and I know he made the world a better place for those who knew him.”
