A Whale of A Weekend

by Dave Stockdill

On Friday February 2, 1990, Bill Nicks, Larry Gross and I met Bob Whaley at the Woodbine Recreation Area to paddle some of the tributaries of the Gauley River. I knew it was going to be a great weekend but I had no idea it would be as great as it was.

As we set the shuttle for the Lower Cranberry, Bob suggested a new method of stacking four solo canoes on one vehicle. The first boat is centered on the racks upside down. Then two boats are put up, one on each side of the bottom boat. These are also upside down with one gunwale on the rack and the other gunwale resting on the bottom of the center boat. The outer boats are secured with the lines attached to the center of the racks. Now the fourth boat can be put up in the center of the two outer also upside down. Two lines are then added over the top. The painters of the outside boats are tied to opposite sides of the vehicle making a secure package. Having the painter lines running diagonally also reduces the visual obstruction for the driver. By the end of the weekend we had mastered this loading technique which also works well with three boats.

The Friday morning run was a 9-mile class II - IV warm-up run on the Lower Cranberry. Friday afternoon we paddled the middle section of the Cranberry. This class III- V run would normally be the climax of a paddling trip but for this weekend it was just the beginning. Saturday after finding the Williams to low too paddle we settled for the Cherry. The Cherry has nice whitewater if you can ignore the trash along the banks and the sewerage treatment plant in Richwood.

Saturday afternoon we east to camp intending to paddle Second Creek on Sunday. We ended up camping in the George Washington National Forest, the closest freebie campsite available. As we set up camp and prepared supper, Bob mentioned another option. Just a couple hundred yards from our campsite was Jerry’s Run. He had run it once before and promised it would be “a trip you will remember the rest of your life.”

Sunday morning, we bank scouted about a half mile of the stream and decided the water level was about right. We broke camp and left for breakfast at Wendy’s in Covington. It rained the whole time we ate and there was some concern that there might be too much water. Luckily the rain let up and the water level remained steady.

Bill and Lary in their Sunburst IIs and Bob and I in our XL-13s put in between Batlick and Brushy Mountains about a quarter mile upstream of our campsite the night before. Jerry’s Run is about 20’ wide below the low water bridge at the put in and narrows to less than 13 feet as it returns to its natural state in the forest. The banks are low and the scenery is beautiful when you get a chance to look. There are few eddies as Jerry’s Run drops 600 feet in 5.5 miles giving an average gradient of 110 feet per mile. In the first quarter mile we encountered two trees in the river that had to be portaged. It was my intention to keep track of the number of strainers and ledges that had to be carried but I lost count after five with many miles ahead of us. The first ledge we came to was a diagonal, river wide, 3 foot drop that posed no problems. After a few more bends in the river we spotted our first horizon line of the morning. Getting out to scout we saw a 15 ft. slide. The right side was dry and the left had a huge boulder at the bottom. Below the slide was a small pool and a 10 ft. waterfall full of jagged boulders, an honest class VI (Risk of Life). Bill and I had an easy carry on an old jeep trail on the left while Larry and Bob carried on the right. After a few more strainers we came to another falls. This one was a seven-foot sliding drop. After a quick boat scout I followed Bob in carrying on the right side and Bill carried on the left. Larry strapped on his helmet and ran the drop making it look effortless. By now I was getting accustomed to doing ‘bank jams’ and jumping out of the boat quickly and it was my turn to run probe. After sliding over two more downed trees we came to a small lake.

The lake is formed by a more than 100 ft. high railroad fill across the valley. As we neared the fill, I could see the opening of the tunnel that Bob had described to us earlier. There is a twenty-foot-wide tunnel blasted through the mountain for the river to flow through. Bob had warned us about the tunnel and described it to us the night before. It was flat with a small drop near the far end.

I watched as Bob lined up and entered the tunnel. I could see Bob and his boat silhouetted by the light at the other end of the tunnel. He was staying close to the right wall. As Bob began to disappear I followed, there were logs along the left wall of the tunnel so I stayed right. Suddenly I saw a log jammed against the right wall. By the time I worked away from it I couldn’t see Bob. I couldn’t see anything. Then I saw Bob’s head and I knew there was a drop ahead. Suddenly dead ahead of me was a ledge, I worked right where there was more water and took the four foot drop. Everything was black except the light at the end of the tunnel with Bob’s silhouette. Then I heard a “YEEEOOOOO” behind me and I knew Bill had come to the ledge. Soon it got lighter and I was out. I caught the eddy on the left just behind Bob and jumped out of my boat, knees shaking, as Bill and Larry caught the eddy. Bob told us that the drop in the middle of the tunnel was not there when he ran it 3 years ago therefor it must have been caused by trees. This tunnel has a very real potential to kill someone. The current is too swift and confined to permit any rescue attempt. If you ever get a chance to run Jerry’s Run bring along a rope and strong lantern and lower a boat down the tunnel to scout before you run it. The life you save may be your own.

Below the tunnel the gradient picked up again. After we rounded a bend and paddled another three-foot ledge a waterfall appeared. This one we didn’t scout. The carry is up a steep hill to the left of the falls. When we got to the top and looked down on it this 20 ft. waterfall looked runnable. It had a glassy smooth tongue just left of center half way down. Below there were some rock outcroppings that looked benign and another seven-foot drop to the bottom. The remainder of the carry over the boulders used in the railroad fill left numerous scars on my new XL-13. A glance up at the falls from midstream revealed that the tongue lead right into the rock outcroppings rather than beside them as it appeared from above.

From the falls down the gradient decreases slightly and branches hang over the river. You soon become adept at laying your chest on your airbags to duck under branches while doing a cross draw to catch a micro-eddy to see what is coming next. After three hours and five and a half miles Jerry’s Run dumps into Dunlop Creek which is huge by comparison.

From Jerry’s Run until just above the Humpback bridge Dunlop Creek is on a plateau. The gradient is good but there are few rapids. There are many foot bridges, at least two of which have been washed out leaving cables in and above the water. There are three dams on Dunlop Creek between Jerry’s Run and the Jackson Fiver. The first one has the right bank washed away making for a smooth, safe bypass of this potential hazard. Just above the Humpback bridge is a six to eight foot high dam that can be run at some water levels. The day we were there it a huge “glassy, Grand Canyon wave” as Bob Whaley put it. A fantastic smooth ride dooowwwn and uuuuppp and down. After passing under ti I-64 bridge there is a series of nice river wide ledges. Then the river makes a right hand U turn around a stone quarry and into the backwater of the third dam. The backwater is short for a big (20 ft) dam, indicating there were probably some good rapids there at one time. After carrying this last dam there were no more rapids until we reached a man-made channel.

Dunlop Creek has also been diverted for the railroad bed. This channel is cut through upstream slanted shale. At this point Dunlop Creek had about 1500 cfs and the bottom of this twenty foot wide channel had a huge nasty hole. The left side of the hole looked easy enough to punch and the approach was clear. I decided to run first. As I got nearer to the hole I could tell I was too far right so I turned the boat left with the intention of paddling to the left and then straightening out the boat. Unfortunately the bow was now in slower current than the stern and I couldn’t straighten out the boat. As I hit the hole sideways I braced for all I was worth. My low brace kept me upright but I couldn’t pull out of the hole. As I switched to a low high brace my right foot slipped off the foot peg. I managed to pull out of the hole but couldn’t roll up. As I was getting out of the boat I saw Larry beside me ready to give me a hand. He had hit the hole perfectly and punched through with no trouble. Next it was Bob’s turn. Bob was lined up right and hit the hole the same place Larry did and it looked like he would have no trouble. But the hungry hole grabbed his boat and pulled him in. Bob braced and worked his way river left nearly out of the hole but the hole would not give up, it pulled Bob in and spat him out keeping his boat to munch on for a while. As Bob’s boat was tumbling in the hole Bill began his approach. When Bill got close enough he could see Bob’s boat in the hole so he took several powerful strokes to punch the hole in the center. When Bill hit the hole he was stopped dead immediately. I have seen many paddlers hit a hole and come part way out but I had never seen anyone hit a hole with such speed and get stopped dead in his tracks. His boat was tossed sideways and he was windowshaded in an instant. Bill’s boat was still tumbling in the hole when he surfaced downstream. Bob and Bill were safely ashore as their boats finally came out of the hole. Before we left we ferried over to the river right side to take a look from there. Not only was the eddy pulsating but it was so powerful that the eddy current had standing waves! This last rapid was a fitting end to a “trip I’ll remember the rest of my life”.
